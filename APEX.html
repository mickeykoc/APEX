Coach's Corner
Methodology, cues, and protocol reference from elite speed coaches.
Charlie Francis
"Everything in a sprint program is in service of the sprint. If it doesn't make you faster, it's overhead. Volumes are medicine — titrated by individual response, not tradition."
Louie Simmons
"Strength is the base that all other physical qualities are built upon. A weak athlete can never be a truly fast athlete. Max effort and dynamic effort are two separate neural demands — train them separately."
Cal Dietz
"The triphasic model follows biology. Eccentric loading creates the spring. Isometric strength holds the spring. Concentric force releases the spring. Violate this order and you leave adaptation on the table."
Tudor Bompa
"Periodization is the science of timing. The body cannot peak continuously. Plan the peaks, plan the recoveries, and the athlete will be ready when it matters most."
Yuri Verkhoshansky
"Plyometric training is shock method — not jump training. The reactive stimulus must exceed the athlete's threshold to drive adaptation. Below threshold is conditioning. Above threshold is speed."
Dan Pfaff
"Technique is not a decoration on top of fitness. Technique IS fitness. A breakdown in mechanics under fatigue reveals the weakest link in the chain — that is where you train next."
01
Popping up too early in acceleration
"Stay patient. Push the ground back — don't stand up. Rise is earned; let it happen naturally after 20m."
02
Training max velocity when fatigued
"Speed work after volume is speed-endurance — a completely different stimulus. Rest 48–72h before flying 30s."
03
Skipping the warm-up CNS primer
"Without Block D, your first sprint is your warm-up sprint — and it goes in the trash."
04
Too much volume on HIGH CNS days
"Three quality sprints are worth more than six tired ones. When speed drops 3% from your day-best, stop."
05
Treating LOW CNS days as easy days
"Tempo at 75% still requires precision. Sloppy posture at 75% programs sloppy posture at 95%."
06
Not rotating max effort movements
"The same movement adapts in 3–6 weeks. Rotate to keep driving adaptation without joint overuse."
07
Ignoring sleep and HRV data
"One bad sleep won't ruin you. Ignoring two in a row will. Your CNS status is a real signal — act on it."
08
Skipping deload weeks
"Deload weeks are where supercompensation happens. The stimulus was last week. This week is when the body upgrades."

Max Effort Rotation
  • Weeks 1–2: Safety bar squat (eccentric focus)
  • Weeks 3–4: Trap bar deadlift (isometric pause)
  • Weeks 5–6: Box squat (dynamic effort)
  • Weeks 7–8: Romanian deadlift → rack pull
  • Weeks 9–10: Squat 1RM test / peak
HIGH CNS Day Recovery
  • 10 min walk immediately post-session (flush lactate)
  • Cold water immersion 10–12°C for 8–10 min (within 30 min)
  • Full-body static stretch 20–30 min (parasympathetic shift)
  • 40–60g protein + carbohydrates within 45 min
  • Sleep 9+ hours in a cool, dark room
  • No high-intensity activity within 36 hours
LOW CNS Day Recovery
  • 5–10 min easy jog cool-down
  • Contrast shower — 3 min hot / 1 min cold × 3 rounds
  • Light mobility: hip flexors, hamstrings, thoracic spine
  • Balanced meal within 60 min
  • Sleep 8+ hours
Weekly Non-Negotiables
  • 2× full-body soft tissue work (foam roll or massage)
  • 7 hrs minimum sleep every night — non-negotiable
  • HRV tracked every morning before standing
  • 30 min low-intensity movement on all OFF days
Red Flag Triggers
  • Morning resting HR ≥8 bpm above baseline
  • HRV drop ≥10% from 7-day average
  • Sleep <6 hrs for 2+ consecutive nights
  • Performance drop >5% across all tests
Response Protocol
  • 1 flag: Add 30 min sleep. Reduce intensity 15%.
  • 2 flags: Convert to LOW CNS day. Remove all sprints.
  • 3+ flags: Full rest day. Walk only. Address root cause.
